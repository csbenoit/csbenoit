Target disks

MAPS targeted five well-known protoplanetary disks for an in-depth chemical exploration. The properties of these disks are listed below and are also available in a convenient python dictionary via our data download page.

12CO 2-1 integrated intensity images from MAPS and millimeter continuum images from DSHARP (IM Lup, AS 209, HD 163296), Huang+20 (GM Aur), and MAPS (MWC 480).  

IM Lup

IM Lup is a young (~1 Myr), Solar-mass star that hosts a massive and radially-extended disk. IM Lup has the largest pebble disk (~260 au) in the DSHARP sample (Huang+18a) and a CO disk out to ~900 au (Cleeves+16). Continuum images reveal the presence of spiral arms in mm grains (Huang+18b), while scattered light observations show a highly flared, multi-ringed disk in micron-sized dust grains (Avenhaus+18). Despite its youth, the IM Lup disk shows evidence of grain growth (Lommen+07). The outer portion of the CO gas disk is diffuse and envelope-like, potentially indicating the presence of an external photoevaporative wind (Haworth+17). IM Lup has been been observed in several molecular lines (Öberg+11) and shows a striking double-ring structure in DCO+ 3-2, due to a change in gas-phase C and O abundance at the pebble disk edge (Öberg+15b). Modeling indicates that CO is underabundant by a factor of ~20 compared to molecular clouds due to missing gas-phase carbon and oxygen (Cleeves+16). No depletions were found in nitrogen, resulting in an elevated gas-phase N/O ratio (Cleeves+18). Spitzer observations showed weak OH and H2O emission in the inner disk, suggesting an intermediate stage of water depletion with some water vapor still present (Banzatti+17).

GM Aur

GM Aur is a ~3-10 Myr T Tauri star in the Taurus-Auriga star-forming region hosting a transition disk, i.e., a disk large with a large inner gap or cavity. The large dust cavity (~35 au) was first inferred from SED modeling (Marsh & Mahoney 92) and later confirmed by direct imaging using mm observations (Hughes+09). High resolution continuum observations at cm and mm wavelengths showed that the outer disk consists of several nested rings surrounded by diffuse continuum emission to ~270 au (Macías+18) and a small inner disk inside this central gap (Huang+20). The GM Aur disk has been observed in several gas tracers, with indications of non-Keplerian CO emission (Dutrey+08) and bright lines associated with photochemistry and small organics (Öberg+10). The MAPS observations show the presence of spectacular, large-scale CO spiral arms (MAPS XIX, Huang+21). Herschel observations show modestly depleted oxygen (Keane+14), but due to its low IR flux, GM Aur was not included in Spitzer surveys (Pontoppidan+10) so its inner disk molecular content is unknown.

AS 209

AS 209 is a young (~1 Myr) T Tauri star in the Ophiuchus star-forming region. The disk is highly structured in mm continuum emission with at least 7 nested rings (e.g., Huang+18a, Guzmán+18), some of which are associated with ongoing planet formation (Favre+19). Scattered light observations show three rings in micron-sized dust grains (Avenhaus+18). The radial gas surface density and CO abundance profiles are also highly structured. Several CO line emission features are spatially related to peaks or gaps in dust emission (Guzmán+18), while continuum and NIR structures are associated with gas surface density perturbations (Teague+18). The AS 209 disk has a rich molecular line inventory at millimeter wavelengths (e.g., Öberg+11, Huang+17, Bergner+18, Bergner+19). Most of the millimeter molecular emission appears depleted towards the disk center but whether this due to dust opacity, excitation, C/N/O abundance, or gas surface density is not yet resolved (but see MAPS XV, Bosman+21). The inner disk chemistry of AS 209 has not been well characterized, with only upper limits on the water reservoir from Herschel (Du+17).

HD 163296

HD 163296 is one of the most well studied Herbig Ae star-disk systems at millimeter wavelengths due to its relative proximity and massive disk. The HD 163296 disk presents multiple features that suggest the presence of planets and ongoing planet formation, including dust rings, azimuthal asymmetries, deviations from Keplerian velocities due to gas pressure variations, "kinks" in the CO emission, and meridional flows (e.g., Isella+16, Pinte+18b, Teague+19). Observations in scattered light at optical and infrared wavelengths also reveal substantial radial substructure (Monnier+17, Muro-Arena+18, Rich+20). HD 163296 has been observed in CO and other molecular tracers at millimeter wavelengths in a number of single-dish, SMA, and ALMA projects (e.g., Thi+04, Qi+11, Öberg+11, Rosenfeld+13, Salinas+17, Pegues+20). Its popularity in previous surveys can be explained by its bright molecular lines, not only in CO isotopologues, but also in HCN, HCO+, deuterated species, and larger organics. The HD 163296 disk is relatively line poor at IR wavelengths (Pontoppidan+10, Salyk+11), but H2O and OH have been detected (Fedele+12, Banzatti+17), revealing a disk atmosphere that is not completely dry.

MWC 480

MWC 480 is a ~7 Myr old Herbig Ae star in the Taurus-Auriga star-forming region. Observations of the MWC 480 mm continuum disk show a centrally-peaked profile (Piétu+06) with two dust rings and low-level emission extending beyond 200 au (Long+18, Liu+19). MWC 480 was included in several early disk chemistry programs (i.e., CID, DISCS; Dutrey+07, Öberg+10, Henning+10, Dutrey+11), which showed that the disk is very bright in some lines, e.g., HCO+ and HCN, but weak in others, including N2H+ and H2CO (e.g., Pegues+20). The disk has also been probed in simple and complex nitriles, deuterated molecules, and sulfur-bearing species (Öberg+15a, Guzmán+15, Huang+17, Le Gal+19) and was the target of an unbiased spectral line survey (Loomis+20), which resulted in detections of several new disk molecules, including C2D and H2CS. The MWC 480 water content was probed by Herschel as part of the WISH key program, but was not detected (van Dishoeck+11). Otherwise, the inner disk chemistry of MWC 480 is unknown.
